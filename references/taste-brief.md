# Taste brief — the four-pole structural rubric (abstract, reusable)

This is the **abstract** rubric the vision-judge applies to a candidate UI alongside the
four-pole picture book (`references/taste-exemplars/`). Each line below describes a pole by its
**structural properties** — depth, cohesion, rhythm, hierarchy-contrast, distinctiveness — NOT
by anyone's content. It is reusable: point it at YOUR OWN exemplars and the same shape holds.

The poles span a band with two failure extremes, a generic baseline, and the good corner. The
weighted-block score is designed to **peak in the sweet spot** — neither a busy nor an empty
page maxes the structural block.

## The four poles

- **too-busy** — Competing focal points; object-soup (orbs, shapes, grids, glows piled on) with
  no single visual priority. Decoration is independent noise, not cohesive support. No depth
  *hierarchy* (everything shouts at one plane). Reads cluttered, not rich. Low cohesion, low
  rhythm despite high element count.

- **too-subtle** — High legibility but flat and empty. One faint gesture (a single soft
  gradient/aurora on white) and otherwise no committed depth, no section-to-section variety,
  every section the same box. Restraint mistaken for design; the band trap — legible-through is
  NOT good. Low depth, low distinctiveness.

- **generic-bad** — Clean and tidy but default. System/Inter font, indigo-on-white, no point of
  view. Nothing is *wrong*; nothing is *committed* either. Adequate legibility, near-zero
  distinctiveness — the template look. The baseline that "looks fine" and goes nowhere.

- **distinctive-good** — ONE committed depth concept carried consistently (not five competing
  ones), strong section rhythm (clear section-to-section variety and pacing), cohesion (the
  decorative layer supports a single direction), and a distinct point of view (a named font
  pairing, a real palette, a committed aesthetic stance). High on all structural dims *together*
  — depth + rhythm + cohesion + hierarchy-contrast + distinctiveness — without tipping into
  busy. This is the corner the loop is climbing toward.

## How to score against this brief

Inventory the candidate's **decorative** and **structural** layers first (see the judge
prompt). Then name **which pole** the candidate is closest to and **why**, grounded in what you
see. Score "which pole" — explicitly NOT "distance to the one good image": a candidate that
commits to its OWN novel direction (different fonts/palette/depth concept from the
`distinctive-good/` exemplars) is still distinctive-good. Distinctiveness rewards committed
NOVELTY, never cloning the exemplar.
