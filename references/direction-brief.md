# Direction brief — the bold-POV mandate (intentionality, not intensity)

This is the reusable rulebook handed to BOTH the explore-mode direction generators AND the refine-mode
synthesize/implement step. Its job is to stop the loop from drawing the same safe, generic page. A
distinctive design is not a *busier* page — it is a *committed* one. Commit to a point of view and
execute it coherently across the whole page. **Intentionality, not intensity.** More layers is not
better; one cohesive idea, fully committed, beats a pile of decorative objects every time.

> The judge scores distinctiveness (dim 11) and cohesion (dim 8). Boldness only counts when it is
> cohesive — incoherent boldness (object-soup) scores LOW. Commit, then restrain.

---

## FORBIDDEN — these are AI-slop defaults; never ship them

- **The default system font / Inter.** No `system-ui`, no `-apple-system`, no bare Inter as the identity.
  A generic system-font page reads as templated and uncommitted. Pick a real, named typeface.
- **Indigo-on-white.** The default indigo (or generic blue) accent on a flat white canvas is the single
  most common AI-generated tell. No indigo-on-white. No single hero accent floating on undifferentiated white.
- **Generic card-on-white.** Rows of identical rounded-corner cards with a hairline border on a white
  background, every section the same box. This is the monotone shell that makes every page look the same.

If your direction contains any of the three above, it is NOT a committed direction — start over.

## DEMANDED — every direction MUST commit to all five

1. **A named font pairing.** A committed display/heading face + a committed body face, chosen by name
   (e.g. a warm serif display + a humanist sans body; a grotesque + a mono). State the pairing explicitly
   and why it fits the posture. The type ramp itself is part of the identity, not an afterthought.
2. **A real palette** — three parts, all required:
   - an **accent** color (the committed identity hue — anything but default indigo/blue-on-white),
   - a **full grey ramp** (a deliberate neutral scale from near-white to near-black, not one flat grey),
   - **semantic** colors (success / warning / danger / info) that belong to the same family.
   A palette is a *system*, not a single accent dropped onto white.
3. **A depth concept.** ONE cohesive idea for how the canvas is NOT flat — elevated planes, layered or
   tinted surfaces, a deliberate elevation ladder, a textured/structured background. One idea, executed
   across the whole page. NOT a pile of shadows, orbs, and gradients. A flat all-white single plane is
   forbidden (it scores low on depth); so is incoherent object-soup (it scores low on cohesion).
4. **A section-rhythm plan.** Down the full-page scroll, sections must DIFFER with deliberate rhythm —
   alternating surface tone/density, at least one full-bleed or break moment, varied heading scale — all
   within ONE vocabulary. The failure mode to avoid is the monotone shell: every section the identical
   box at the identical width separated by a hairline. Plan the rhythm explicitly, section by section.
5. **ONE cohesive content-motion technique.** A single, restrained motion idea tied to the CONTENT
   (e.g. scroll-linked section transitions, a staged reveal, a content-aware parallax) — not five
   unrelated animations. It must respect `prefers-reduced-motion` (go static). One technique, applied
   consistently, is the substance half of the leap.

---

## The test of a committed direction

Could someone describe your page in one sentence that a *different* template could not also satisfy? If
"clean modern landing page with cards" fits, you have NOT committed. "A warm editorial broadsheet with a
terracotta accent, a timeline-ruled experience section, and serif pull-quotes that fade in on scroll" —
THAT is a committed direction. Name the posture, pick the fonts and palette, plan the depth and the
rhythm, choose the one motion, and execute all of it coherently.
